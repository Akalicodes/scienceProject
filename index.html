01

Interphase

The cell prepares for division by replicating its DNA. Each chromosome now has a duplicate copy called a sister chromatid, joined at the centromere. The cell also grows and synthesizes the proteins needed for division.

Key Event: DNA Replication
Result: Sister Chromatids Form
02

Prophase I

Homologous chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) pair up side by side. While paired, they exchange DNA segments through crossing over. This genetic recombination creates variation in offspring.

Key Event: Crossing Over
Result: Genetic Variation
03

Metaphase I

Homologous chromosome pairs align at the cell's equator, forming the metaphase plate. Spindle fibers latch onto each chromosome at the centromere, getting ready to pull them apart.

Key Event: Chromosome Alignment
Structure: Metaphase Plate
04

Anaphase I

Homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles of the cell. Note: Sister chromatids remain attached at their centromeres during this phase.

Key Event: Homolog Separation
Important: Reduction Division
05

Telophase I

Chromosomes arrive at opposite poles of the cell. The cell prepares for cytokinesis.

Key Event: Chromosome Decondensation
Next: Cytokinesis I
06

Cytokinesis I

The cell divides into two daughter cells. Each cell now has half the original number of chromosomes (haploid). However, each chromosome still consists of two sister chromatids requiring a second division.

Key Event: Cell Division
Result: Two Haploid Cells (2n‚Üín)
Chromosome State: Still Duplicated
07

Interkinesis

A brief resting phase between the two meiotic divisions. The cells rest briefly before entering meiosis II.

Duration: Brief Rest Period
DNA Replication: NONE ‚ùå
Purpose: Prepare for Meiosis II
08

Prophase II

Meiosis II begins. New spindle fibers form in both cells. Chromosomes condense again.

Key Event: Spindle Formation
Note: No DNA Replication
09

Metaphase II

Individual chromosomes align at the metaphase plate in both cells. Preparing to separate sister chromatids.

Key Event: Chromosome Alignment
Similar to: Mitosis Metaphase
10

Anaphase II

Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles of each cell. Once separated, each chromatid is now considered an individual chromosome.

Key Event: Chromatid Separation
Result: Individual Chromosomes
11

Telophase II + Cytokinesis II

Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes. Cytokinesis divides both cells again, producing four haploid gametes. Each gamete is genetically unique due to crossing over and independent assortment.

Key Event: Final Division
Result: 4 Unique Gametes

Genetic Variation Explained

üîÄ

Crossing Over

Mechanism:

During Prophase I, homologous chromosomes (maternal and paternal) pair closely and exchange DNA segments at points called chiasmata. This process shuffles genetic material between chromosome pairs.

Significance:

Crossing over creates new combinations of alleles. Without it, each chromosome would be inherited intact from one parent. This recombination produces genetic diversity within populations.

Greater genetic variation allows species to adapt to environmental changes. This is why siblings inherit different combinations of parental traits.

2-3 crossover events per chromosome pair

Independent Assortment

Mechanism:

During Metaphase I, homologous pairs align randomly at the metaphase plate. The orientation of each pair is independent‚Äîmaternal or paternal chromosomes can face either pole. This randomness determines which chromosomes end up in each gamete.

Significance:

Each gamete receives a random assortment of maternal and paternal chromosomes. This follows Mendel's law of independent assortment.

In humans (23 chromosome pairs), independent assortment alone creates over 8 million possible combinations per gamete. This exponential variation is fundamental to genetic diversity.

8.4 Million+ possible chromosome combinations

The Big Picture

Crossing over and independent assortment together can produce over 70 trillion different gamete combinations. When fertilization occurs, the genetic possibilities become nearly infinite. This variation is the foundation of evolution and why each person is genetically unique.

Quick Summary

Mitosis makes exact copies for growth and healing.
Meiosis makes unique sperm/egg cells for reproduction.

Both processes are essential: mitosis maintains your body, while meiosis creates genetic diversity in offspring.
